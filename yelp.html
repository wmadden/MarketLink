    I needed to get a book for a birthday present. I thought, 'Where is the best place to buy a classy book, an interesting book and where can I go that will have the biggest range?' The answer was simple. Jupiter. I would fly to Jupiter.

    Wait, wrong. I went to Kinokuniya. The bookshop with the hardest name to spell. I took the escalator up to the top floor and walked into the expansive book shop.

    'There must be literally millions of books in here.' I said to myself, 'How many trees and how many words is that? Lots!' I said in call and response to myself once more. (Editors Note: If you had a friend you wouldn't have to talk to yourself all the time.) (Seaton's Note: Will you be my friend?) (Editors Note: No.)

    I went to the gardening section and began to browse. There were so many books to choose from. Eventually I decided on one, went to the stationary section, chose some wrapping paper and then walked over to the checkout.

    There's a proverb that says that it's easy to get lost in a good book, that's true, but it's also true that it's easy to get lost in a good book shop. Not for lack of direction and cartography, but because there are so many books and the place is so large. But if there was a map, it would make life easier and DATA: if there was a compass, maybe it wouldn't make it easier but at least we'd know which way was north.

    Devil's advocate.

    Yes, enormous. Yes, carries everything. Yes, cavernous. In the sense of vast, soulless spaces where your voice echoes off into the distance, never to be heard by another human ear.

    I am the bookiest of booky-possible people in a parallel universe of entirely book obsessed people. But I can't warm to Kinokuniya. The staff are few and far between, and I suspect from previous bookshop experience must be seriously overworked to keep such a mammoth shop going. And yes, I've found many an unusual item on shelf. But I'd rather buy it from a local bookstore staffed by knowledgeable people respected for their customer service and place in the local community. Call me old-fashioned. Fine by me.

    Walking the empty rows of bookshelves (an activity I usually delight in to ludicrous levels) here, I keep waiting for something lurking in the emptiness to reach out and grab me. I'm still waiting.

    I could literally spend hours here, it's one of my favourite book store in the whole world (even the online world) The journey down George street, and into the Galleries Victoria shopping area is not complete without a visit, and a coffee while you're there.

    You walk into an experience, with some live readings, autograph sessions by famous authors, and some of the hard to find favourites ranging from fiction to historic designs from the Bauhaus movement, through to medical espionage and self help books for dealing with an indecisive hunger. If you have a topic in mind, they have a great book for it.

    Not to mentioned (not of obvious) this place is huge, gigantic, suffering from elephantiasis. Tell your friends where you're going, and to call the authorities if you don't come back, either you'll be helplessly lost, or in a book coma on the floor, thrusting back and forth, foaming from the mouth protecting your mountain of books like a possessed book merchant.

    • Amy A.

    Sydney New South Wales

    23/10/2011 2 photos

    Kinokuniya is a mega book store with levels and levels of books to browse from. Their website boasts having the largest collection (300,000) books in a number of languages, including English, Japanese, French and German. They also stock a large collection of Japanese Manga and graphic novels. The book store is comfortable to not only browse but to read in, and their helpful staff are quick to recommend favourites. They even signpost reviews of different books so you don't need to spend hours and hours reading the first page of each book.

    If you're after a particular title they have these little handy Kino search stations. Simply touch screen the title you're after and a range of relevant titles will pop up. Most of them are in stock, but you may need to have them ordered in. Since the store is so huge, you can even print off a miniature map to lead you to where your book can be found! Kinokuniya also launches a number of book signings and author talks that makes every other day there that DATA: much more fun.

    I really want to give Kinokuniya five stars as it is easily the most extensive bookshop in Sydney, I would be quite content living in the art and design sections which are inspiring and mind blowing.

I purchased numerous Frommes travel guides for the various countries in my trip. Once I arrived in Shanghai and could not work out the train lines, I discovered the train maps in my book were out of date and my b DATA: ooks for Shanghai and Beijing were both missing train lines on the maps as the books were over two years old and not the latest editions. Also several places I had used the books to try and visit were no longer there. A bookshop the size of Kinokuniya should really stock the latest editions of travel books for this reason. It was frustrating.

    Other than that I love the shop.

    Kinokuniya is my version of heaven, or at the very least my solace in the CBD. Ascending the escalators in The Galleries Victoria, I can already feel myself starting to relax. In mere moments I'll be enveloped by rows upon rows of shiny books - for this is a store big enough to forget your wor DATA: ries and just get lost.

    With Borders biting the dust here, Kinokuniya feels like the last bastion of a bygone era. We're all buying our books online these days, or eschewing paperbacks altogether in favour of e-readers, so there's something wonderfully bold about this vast bookstore. It even sports a cafe.

    Whether you're looking for a graphic novel, some Star Wars paraphernalia, super cute Japanese stationery or a beautifully glossy coffee table book, Kinokuniya has well and truly got you covered. Or perhaps you just want to roam shelves as I do, feeling a little nostalgic for the days when bookstores were beloved, and bigger than Ben Hur.

    Books Kinokuniya is pro DATA: bably the greatest bookstore known to mankind. I know a lot of people like the comfort of a cosy and quaint little store, bundled up with a cup of coffee and the smell of yellowy pulp. Not me. Just like rooting for the New York Yankees or movies where the underdog doesn't win, I like my bookstores mammoth. Build 'em big and with bright light, aisles and aisles of books, more than I'll ever have time to read. Make me feel alone on a little raft in an ocean of knowledge, deeper and darker than the hearts of man.

    For those worried that Kinokuniya will be a THX-1138 type of white isolating hell, rest assured. The architects have seen fit to install a number of large windows with benches, allowing you to sit and ponder as you look across Town Hall and the Queen Victoria Building. The selection of titles in this store is astounding, with dedicated fiction, non-fiction, non-fic subject specific, film, music, design, comic book, and photography sections. Naturally, one would think that a store of this proportions would bring a price of commensurate rate. Well, fret no more - in my experience, Kinokuniya has been exceedingly affordable, generally below the going rate at Angus & Robertson and Dymocks. The staff are young and knowledgeable with at least three small kiosks dotted across the store in addition to the main counter and a handful of touchscreen kiosks, allowing customers to search the database at their own conveni DATA: ence.

    (For those trying to figure out the name, my best efforts have led me to deduce that it is pronounced kino-kun-iya, but I prefer to call it Books K).

    Kinokuniya Bookstores are heaven for those who love books. There are Kinokuniya stores all over the world and have been my favourite since I was much younger and living in Asia. A trip to Kinokuniya was a special treat and I have very fond memories spending hours pouring over books and magazines before making a purchase. Staff are only too happy for you to spend hours entertaining yourself. While the seats aren't plentiful, DATA: there is space to sit and read.
